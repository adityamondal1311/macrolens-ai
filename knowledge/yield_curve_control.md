# Yield Curve Control (YCC)

## What It Is
Yield Curve Control is a monetary policy tool where a central bank targets a specific yield level on a particular bond maturity — rather than just targeting short-term interest rates. The central bank commits to buying unlimited quantities of bonds to defend the yield target, effectively capping the yield at a chosen level.

## How It Works
Normal monetary policy targets the overnight rate (e.g., Fed Funds Rate). YCC extends control further along the yield curve:

```
Central Bank announces: "10Y yield will not exceed X%"
If yield rises above X% → Central bank buys bonds → Yield is pushed back down
The threat of unlimited buying is itself often sufficient to anchor yields
```

## The Bank of Japan's YCC Program
The only major central bank to implement full YCC. The BoJ introduced it in September 2016 targeting:
- Short-term rate: -0.1%
- 10-year JGB yield: 0% (±0.1% band initially)

The band was gradually widened under pressure:
- December 2022: Band widened to ±0.5%
- July 2023: Band effectively widened to ±1.0%
- March 2024: YCC formally abandoned, BoJ hiked rates

## Why Japan Used YCC
Japan faced decades of deflation and stagnant growth. YCC was designed to:
1. Keep borrowing costs ultralow across the entire curve (not just short end)
2. Steepen the yield curve to help bank profitability
3. Force inflation higher by signaling unlimited monetary commitment
4. Allow fiscal stimulus without rising debt servicing costs

## The Problems with YCC

### Currency Depreciation
Keeping yields artificially low while other central banks hike causes massive interest rate differentials. Capital flows out seeking higher yields, weakening the yen dramatically. USD/JPY went from 115 to 150 during the 2022 hiking cycle as the BoJ maintained YCC while the Fed hiked aggressively.

### Loss of Market Function
When the BoJ buys unlimited bonds to defend the yield cap, it can end up owning virtually all outstanding bonds in certain maturities. Secondary market trading ceases. Price discovery is destroyed. At peak YCC, the BoJ owned over 50% of Japan's government bond market.

### Imported Inflation
Yen weakness from YCC makes imports more expensive. Japan, which imports most of its energy and food, imported significant inflation — the opposite of what the deflationary environment once required.

### Eventual Forced Exit
Markets can attack YCC by selling bonds aggressively, forcing the central bank to buy ever-larger quantities. This is unsustainable — the BoJ spent record sums defending yield caps in 2022-2023 before eventually abandoning YCC.

## YCC and Global Markets
BoJ's YCC had significant global implications:
- **JGB yield as global anchor:** Ultralow JGB yields suppressed global bond yields through portfolio substitution
- **Yen carry trade:** Low Japanese rates funded carry trades globally
- **When BoJ widened/abandoned YCC:** Global bonds sold off (JGB yields rising raised global yields), yen surged, carry trades unwound

## Could Other Central Banks Use YCC?
The Fed briefly discussed YCC during COVID but did not implement it. The RBA (Reserve Bank of Australia) implemented YCC targeting 3-year yields during COVID but abandoned it in 2021 when markets challenged the cap.

The lesson: YCC works when defended credibly, but eventual exit is disruptive. The longer YCC is maintained, the more distorted markets become and the more violent the eventual normalization.

## Bottom Line
YCC is a radical monetary tool that trades short-term yield control for long-term market distortion and currency weakness. The BoJ's YCC experiment was the most significant monetary policy experiment of the 2010s-2020s. Its unwinding will continue to affect global bond and currency markets for years.
