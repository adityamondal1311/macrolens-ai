# Carry Trade

## What It Is
The carry trade is one of the most popular strategies in global macro — borrow money in a low-interest-rate currency, convert it to a high-interest-rate currency, invest at the higher rate, and pocket the interest rate differential (the "carry").

```
Carry = High-Yield Currency Rate - Low-Yield Funding Currency Rate
```

## Classic Example
- Borrow Japanese yen at 0.1% interest rate
- Convert yen to Australian dollars
- Invest in Australian government bonds yielding 4.5%
- Earn 4.4% carry (before currency moves)

If the AUD/JPY exchange rate stays stable, you earn 4.4% risk-free. This is the carry trade in its simplest form.

## Funding Currencies (Low Rate, Borrow Here)
- **Japanese Yen (JPY):** The most popular carry funding currency. Japan maintained near-zero rates for decades.
- **Swiss Franc (CHF):** Historically low rates, safe haven currency
- **Euro (EUR):** Periods of negative rates (2014-2022) made it a funding currency

## Target Currencies (High Rate, Invest Here)
- **Australian Dollar (AUD):** Historically higher rates, commodity-linked
- **New Zealand Dollar (NZD):** Higher rates, risk-sensitive
- **Brazilian Real (BRL):** Very high rates but volatile
- **Mexican Peso (MXN):** High rates, relatively stable
- **Indian Rupee (INR):** High rates, large economy
- **Turkish Lira (TRY):** Very high rates, but extreme volatility makes carry dangerous

## Why Carry Trades Work
Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP) theory says exchange rates should move to offset interest rate differentials — eliminating carry profits. In practice, UIP consistently fails in the short to medium run. High-yield currencies tend to appreciate rather than depreciate against expectations — the "forward premium puzzle." This means carry traders often earn both the interest differential AND currency appreciation.

## Carry Trade Risks

### Currency Risk
The most obvious risk. If the target currency depreciates by more than the carry earned, the trade loses money. A 4.4% carry is wiped out by a 5% AUD depreciation.

### Carry Unwind / Sudden Reversal
The most dangerous risk. Carry trades are crowded — many investors run similar positions. When risk sentiment deteriorates, everyone tries to exit simultaneously:
1. Investors sell high-yield currencies
2. Buy back funding currencies (yen, franc)
3. Funding currencies surge, target currencies collapse
4. Losses are immediate and severe

JPY carry unwinds are legendary for their violence — the yen can appreciate 10-15% in days during crisis events.

### Leverage Risk
Carry trades are typically leveraged, amplifying both gains and losses. A seemingly modest 2-3% carry can be leveraged 10x, creating significant risk.

### Volatility Regime Change
Carry trades are short volatility by nature. When volatility spikes (VIX surges), carry trades get crushed. The relationship between VIX and carry trade performance is strongly negative.

## Famous Carry Trade Unwinds

### 2008 Global Financial Crisis
The yen carry trade unwound violently. JPY appreciated 30% against AUD in months. Leveraged carry traders faced catastrophic losses.

### 2015 China Devaluation
Risk-off triggered by China's surprise yuan devaluation caused sharp carry unwinds across EM currencies.

### August 2024 BoJ Rate Hike
When the Bank of Japan unexpectedly raised rates, JPY carry trades unwound violently — the yen surged, Japanese stocks fell, and global markets sold off sharply as leveraged carry positions were liquidated.

## Carry Trade as a Risk Barometer
The yen carry trade is a real-time indicator of global risk appetite:
- AUD/JPY rising = risk-on, carry trade in favor
- AUD/JPY falling = risk-off, carry trade unwinding
- Sudden JPY strengthening = global risk event, watch for contagion

## Measuring Carry
The G10 carry index tracks a basket of long high-yield / short low-yield G10 currencies. It tends to trend upward slowly during calm periods and crash sharply during risk events.

## Bottom Line
Carry is free money until it isn't. The trade works the vast majority of the time in calm markets. But when it unwinds, it does so with extreme velocity and is often correlated with other risk-off moves. Sizing and risk management are critical. Always know where the exits are.
