# Bond Yields

## What They Are
A bond yield is the return an investor receives on a bond. When bond prices rise, yields fall. When bond prices fall, yields rise. This inverse relationship is fundamental to all macro analysis.

## Types of Yields
- **Nominal Yield:** The stated coupon rate divided by the bond price.
- **Real Yield:** Nominal yield minus expected inflation. The true return after inflation.
- **Yield to Maturity (YTM):** Total return if the bond is held until maturity, accounting for price paid, coupons, and face value.

## The US Treasury Market
US Treasuries are the world's most important bond market and the global risk-free rate benchmark. Key maturities traders watch:
- **2-Year Treasury:** Most sensitive to Fed policy expectations
- **10-Year Treasury:** The benchmark rate. Affects mortgage rates, corporate borrowing, equity valuations
- **30-Year Treasury:** Long-duration, most sensitive to long-term inflation expectations

## Why Bond Yields Move

### Fed Policy
The Fed directly controls the short end of the curve (overnight rates). When the Fed hikes, 2Y yields rise immediately. The 10Y and 30Y respond based on where markets expect rates to be over the long run.

### Inflation Expectations
If markets expect higher future inflation, they demand higher yields to compensate. The 10Y yield can be decomposed as:
```
10Y Yield = Real Rate + Inflation Expectations
```

### Growth Expectations
Strong economic growth raises yields because it increases demand for capital and reduces the likelihood of rate cuts.

### Safe Haven Flows
In times of crisis, investors flood into Treasuries, pushing prices up and yields down regardless of fundamentals.

### Supply and Demand
The US government issuing more debt (fiscal deficits) increases bond supply, which pushes yields higher if demand doesn't keep pace.

## Real Yields and Their Importance
Real yields (nominal yield minus breakeven inflation) are arguably more important than nominal yields. When real yields are deeply negative, it means holding bonds guarantees a loss in purchasing power. This environment is:
- Bullish for gold
- Bullish for equities (especially growth)
- Bearish for the dollar

When real yields rise sharply, the opposite occurs — growth stocks and gold sell off hard.

## Bond Yields and Equities
The equity risk premium (ERP) is the excess return stocks offer over bonds:
```
ERP = Earnings Yield (1/PE ratio) - 10Y Treasury Yield
```
When the 10Y yield rises, bonds become more attractive relative to equities, compressing equity valuations. This is why rising yields often trigger equity selloffs, especially in high-multiple growth stocks.

## Bottom Line
The 10-year Treasury yield is the most important price in global finance. It anchors mortgage rates, corporate credit, and equity discount rates. Every macro trader watches it daily.
