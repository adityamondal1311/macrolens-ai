# Liquidity and Money Supply

## What Is Liquidity?
In macro, liquidity has two meanings:
1. **Market liquidity:** How easily an asset can be bought or sold without moving its price
2. **Monetary liquidity:** The amount of money and credit circulating in the financial system

Macro traders focus primarily on monetary liquidity — it is the fuel for asset price inflation and the brake for asset price deflation.

## Money Supply Measures

### M0 (Monetary Base)
Physical currency in circulation plus bank reserves held at the central bank. Directly controlled by the Fed. Also called "high-powered money."

### M1
M0 plus demand deposits (checking accounts). The most liquid money — immediately spendable.

### M2
M1 plus savings deposits, money market funds, and small time deposits. The most watched broad money supply measure. When M2 grows rapidly, it signals excess liquidity in the system — historically correlated with future inflation.

```
M2 Growth Rate → Leading indicator for inflation with 12-18 month lag
```

M2 contracted in 2022-2023 for the first time since the 1930s, signaling significant monetary tightening.

### M3 / Global Liquidity
Broader measures including large institutional deposits and repo agreements. The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) tracks global liquidity — the aggregate money supply across all major central banks.

## Global Liquidity Cycle
The most important macro cycle for asset prices. Global liquidity is driven by:
- Central bank balance sheet expansion/contraction (QE/QT)
- Credit creation by commercial banks
- Dollar strength/weakness (dollar is the global funding currency)
- Cross-border capital flows

**When global liquidity expands:**
- Risk assets rally (equities, crypto, EM, high yield)
- Volatility falls
- Credit spreads tighten
- Dollar tends to weaken

**When global liquidity contracts:**
- Risk assets sell off
- Volatility spikes
- Credit spreads widen
- Dollar tends to strengthen

## Central Bank Balance Sheets
The aggregate size of major central bank balance sheets (Fed, ECB, BoJ, PBoC) is a proxy for global liquidity. Macro traders track this closely:

- **Fed Balance Sheet:** Peaked at ~$9 trillion in 2022, being reduced via QT
- **BoJ Balance Sheet:** Largest relative to GDP — holds over 50% of Japan's government bond market
- **Combined G4 Balance Sheet:** The master liquidity variable — when it expands, risk assets globally tend to rally

## The Liquidity Cycle and Asset Prices
Michael Howell of CrossBorder Capital has documented that approximately 70% of asset price movements can be explained by global liquidity cycles — more than economic fundamentals.

**Liquidity leading indicator sequence:**
1. Central bank balance sheet expands
2. Bank reserves rise
3. Banks extend more credit
4. Money supply grows
5. Financial conditions loosen
6. Asset prices rise (with 3-6 month lag)
7. Real economy eventually follows

## Bank Credit Creation
Commercial banks multiply base money through lending. When a bank makes a loan, it creates new deposits — money supply expands without central bank action. This credit creation cycle is procyclical:
- In expansions: banks lend freely, money supply grows, asset prices rise
- In recessions: banks tighten lending standards, money supply contracts, asset prices fall

## Financial Conditions Indices
The Fed and Goldman Sachs publish Financial Conditions Indices (FCI) that aggregate:
- Short-term interest rates
- Long-term bond yields
- Credit spreads
- Equity prices
- Dollar strength

Tighter financial conditions = less effective liquidity = headwind for growth and asset prices.

## Repo Market and Overnight Liquidity
The repurchase agreement (repo) market is where short-term liquidity is distributed in the financial system. Banks and financial institutions borrow overnight using Treasuries as collateral. When repo markets seize up (as in September 2019), it signals acute liquidity stress — the Fed must intervene immediately.

## Bottom Line
Liquidity is the tide that lifts or sinks all boats. Follow global central bank balance sheets and M2 growth as leading indicators for risk appetite. When liquidity is expanding, lean long risk assets. When it's contracting, defensive positioning is warranted.
