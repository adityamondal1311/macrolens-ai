# Inflation

## What It Is
Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises over time, eroding purchasing power. It is measured primarily by CPI (Consumer Price Index) and PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures).

## Why It Matters in Macro Trading
Inflation is the single most important variable driving central bank policy. Every major asset class — bonds, equities, currencies, commodities — reacts to inflation data.

## Key Inflation Indicators
- **CPI (Consumer Price Index):** Measures price changes in a basket of consumer goods. The headline number includes food and energy; core CPI strips these out.
- **PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures):** The Fed's preferred inflation measure. Broader than CPI and adjusts for consumer substitution behavior.
- **PPI (Producer Price Index):** Measures wholesale prices. Often a leading indicator for CPI since producer costs eventually pass through to consumers.
- **Breakeven Inflation Rate:** Derived from TIPS vs nominal Treasury spreads. Reflects market expectations of future inflation.

## How Inflation Affects Markets

### Bonds
Higher inflation is the enemy of bonds. It erodes the real value of fixed coupon payments. When inflation rises, bond yields rise and bond prices fall. Long-duration bonds are hit hardest because their cash flows are further in the future.

### Equities
Moderate inflation is acceptable for equities. High inflation hurts in two ways:
1. The Fed raises rates, increasing the discount rate applied to future earnings
2. Input costs rise, compressing profit margins

Growth stocks with distant cash flows are hit harder than value stocks with near-term earnings.

### Currencies
Higher inflation typically weakens a currency over time (purchasing power parity), but in the short run, if inflation forces the central bank to raise rates, the currency can strengthen due to higher yield differentials attracting capital flows.

### Commodities
Commodities like gold and oil are often used as inflation hedges. Gold particularly tends to rise when real interest rates (nominal rates minus inflation) fall into negative territory.

## Inflation Regimes Macro Traders Watch
- **Disinflation:** Inflation falling but still positive. Generally bullish for bonds.
- **Deflation:** Prices falling. Extremely dangerous — triggers debt deflation spirals.
- **Stagflation:** High inflation + low growth. The worst environment. Bonds and equities fall together. Commodities outperform.
- **Reflation:** Inflation rising from low levels. Typically bullish for cyclicals, commodities, and EM assets.

## Historical Reference
The 1970s stagflation in the US was caused by oil supply shocks combined with loose monetary policy. The Fed under Volcker ended it by raising rates to 20% in 1981, causing a severe recession but breaking inflation expectations permanently.

## Bottom Line
Watch CPI and PCE monthly. Any surprise above consensus moves bond yields immediately. The Fed's 2% inflation target is the anchor of all modern monetary policy.
