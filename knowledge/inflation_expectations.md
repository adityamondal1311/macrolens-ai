# Inflation Expectations

## Why Expectations Matter More Than Actual Inflation
In macro trading, expected future inflation is more important than current inflation. Central banks explicitly target inflation expectations — if expectations become "unanchored" (people expect high inflation to persist), it becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. Workers demand higher wages, companies raise prices preemptively, and inflation embeds itself into the economy.

This is why the Fed watches surveys and market-based inflation measures obsessively, not just the CPI print.

## Breakeven Inflation Rate
The most important market-based measure of inflation expectations.

```
Breakeven Inflation = Nominal Treasury Yield - TIPS Yield
```

For example, if the 10Y Treasury yields 4.5% and the 10Y TIPS yields 2.0%, the 10-year breakeven inflation rate is 2.5%. This means the market expects average inflation of 2.5% per year over the next 10 years.

Key breakeven tenors macro traders watch:
- **2Y Breakeven:** Short-term inflation expectations, most sensitive to energy prices
- **5Y Breakeven:** Medium-term expectations
- **5Y5Y Forward Breakeven:** Expected inflation from year 5 to year 10. The Fed's favorite market-based measure because it strips out near-term noise and reflects long-run expectations.

When breakevens rise above 2.5%, markets are pricing in inflation above the Fed's target — hawkish pressure increases. When breakevens collapse, deflation fears emerge.

## TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)
US government bonds whose principal adjusts with CPI. If CPI rises 3%, the TIPS principal rises 3%, protecting the holder's real purchasing power.

- **TIPS yield = real yield** (return above inflation)
- When TIPS yields are negative, investors are paying for inflation protection
- Deeply negative TIPS yields = extremely bullish for gold
- Rising TIPS yields = bearish for gold, growth stocks, and EM assets

TIPS are the direct instrument for expressing inflation views in the bond market.

## Inflation Swaps
Derivatives where one party pays a fixed rate and receives actual realized CPI inflation. Used by:
- Hedge funds to speculate on inflation direction
- Corporations to hedge inflation risk in their cost structure
- Pension funds to match inflation-linked liabilities

The inflation swap rate is another measure of market inflation expectations, often used alongside breakevens as a cross-check.

## How Expectations Affect Bond Markets
Inflation expectations directly drive the nominal yield:

```
Nominal Yield = Real Yield + Inflation Expectations
```

When inflation expectations rise:
- Nominal bond yields rise (bond prices fall)
- The Fed faces pressure to hike rates
- Long-duration bonds underperform most severely
- TIPS outperform nominal Treasuries

When inflation expectations fall:
- Nominal bond yields fall (bond prices rise)
- Fed has room to cut or pause
- Long-duration bonds rally strongly

## The Fisher Effect
The economic relationship that nominal interest rates move one-for-one with inflation expectations in the long run. Named after economist Irving Fisher:

```
Nominal Rate = Real Rate + Expected Inflation
```

In practice, if markets expect inflation to rise 1%, nominal yields should eventually rise by roughly 1%, holding real rates constant.

## Survey-Based Measures
In addition to market-based measures, the Fed watches survey expectations:
- **University of Michigan 5-10 Year Inflation Expectations:** Consumer survey, watched for signs of unanchoring
- **NY Fed Survey of Consumer Expectations:** Monthly, detailed breakdown
- **Philadelphia Fed Survey of Professional Forecasters:** Economist consensus

When survey expectations diverge significantly from market-based breakevens, it signals uncertainty about the inflation outlook.

## Bottom Line
Breakeven inflation rates and TIPS yields are the real-time market verdict on inflation. The 5Y5Y forward breakeven is the single number the Fed watches most carefully for signs that long-run expectations are drifting from 2%. When it moves, markets move.
