# Central Banks

## What They Are
Central banks are institutions that manage a nation's currency, money supply, and interest rates. They are the most powerful actors in global financial markets. A single statement from a central bank governor can move trillions of dollars in assets within seconds.

## The Major Central Banks

### Federal Reserve (Fed) — USA
The most important central bank in the world. Because the US dollar is the global reserve currency, Fed decisions ripple through every asset class globally.
- **Mandate:** Dual mandate — price stability (2% inflation) and maximum employment
- **Key Rate:** Federal Funds Rate (overnight lending rate between banks)
- **Key Events:** FOMC meetings (8 per year), Jackson Hole Symposium (August)
- **Key People:** Fed Chair, FOMC voting members

### European Central Bank (ECB) — Eurozone
- **Mandate:** Single mandate — price stability (2% inflation)
- **Key Rate:** Main Refinancing Rate, Deposit Facility Rate
- **Complexity:** Must balance 20 different economies with one monetary policy. This structural tension is a constant source of market volatility.

### Bank of Japan (BoJ) — Japan
- **Known For:** Decades of ultra-loose monetary policy, yield curve control (YCC), negative interest rates
- **Key Dynamic:** The BoJ's policy divergence from the Fed creates massive yen carry trade flows. When BoJ tightens, the yen rallies sharply and carry trades unwind globally.

### Bank of England (BoE) — UK
- **Mandate:** 2% inflation target
- **Key Dynamic:** Post-Brexit complexity and sensitivity to sterling weakness feeding import inflation.

### People's Bank of China (PBoC) — China
- **Key Dynamic:** Not fully independent from government. Manages the yuan within a band. Chinese credit cycles have massive commodity demand implications.

## How Central Banks Move Markets

### Rate Hikes (Tightening)
- Bond yields rise (especially short end)
- Currency strengthens (higher yields attract capital)
- Equities fall (higher discount rate, slower growth)
- Gold falls (real yields rise)
- Credit spreads widen

### Rate Cuts (Easing)
- Bond yields fall
- Currency weakens
- Equities rise
- Gold rises (real yields fall)
- Credit spreads tighten

## Quantitative Easing (QE) vs Quantitative Tightening (QT)
- **QE:** Central bank buys bonds with newly created money, expanding its balance sheet. Pushes yields down, inflates asset prices.
- **QT:** Central bank shrinks its balance sheet by letting bonds mature without reinvesting or actively selling. Removes liquidity from the system.

## Forward Guidance
Central banks communicate future policy intentions to manage market expectations. This is as powerful as actual rate moves. Phrases like "higher for longer" or "data dependent" shift market pricing dramatically.

## The Reaction Function
Macro traders try to model the central bank's reaction function — how it will respond to economic data. The Taylor Rule is a framework:
```
Target Rate = Neutral Rate + 0.5(Inflation Gap) + 0.5(Output Gap)
```

## Bottom Line
Never fight the Fed. Central bank policy is the dominant macro force. Understanding where a central bank is in its cycle — hiking, pausing, cutting — is the foundation of all macro positioning.
