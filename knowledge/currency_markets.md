# Currency Markets

## What They Are
The foreign exchange (FX) market is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with over $7 trillion traded daily. Unlike stocks, currencies trade 24 hours a day, 5 days a week across global financial centers.

## Key Currency Pairs

### Major Pairs (vs USD)
- **EUR/USD:** Most traded pair in the world. Euro vs Dollar.
- **USD/JPY:** Dollar vs Japanese Yen. Key carry trade currency.
- **GBP/USD:** British Pound vs Dollar. "Cable."
- **USD/CHF:** Dollar vs Swiss Franc. Safe haven pair.
- **USD/CAD:** Dollar vs Canadian Dollar. Correlated with oil prices.
- **AUD/USD:** Australian Dollar vs Dollar. Correlated with commodities and China growth.
- **NZD/USD:** New Zealand Dollar vs Dollar. Risk-sensitive commodity currency.

### Cross Pairs
Currency pairs that don't include the USD. EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY, EUR/GBP etc.

## The DXY (Dollar Index)
The DXY measures the US dollar against a basket of 6 currencies: EUR (57.6%), JPY (13.6%), GBP (11.9%), CAD (9.1%), SEK (4.2%), CHF (3.6%).

The DXY is the macro trader's shorthand for dollar strength/weakness. A rising DXY is generally:
- Bearish for commodities (priced in dollars)
- Bearish for emerging markets (dollar-denominated debt becomes harder to service)
- Bearish for US multinational earnings (overseas revenue worth less in dollar terms)
- Bullish for US import prices falling (deflationary)

## What Drives Currency Movements

### Interest Rate Differentials
The most important driver. Capital flows to higher-yielding currencies:
```
Higher rates → Currency appreciates
Lower rates → Currency depreciates
```

### Growth Differentials
Stronger economic growth attracts investment, boosting currency demand.

### Current Account Balance
Countries with current account surpluses (export more than import) have structurally stronger currencies. Japan and Germany are examples.

### Risk Sentiment
In risk-off environments, capital flees to safe haven currencies: USD, JPY, CHF.
In risk-on environments, capital flows to high-yielding EM currencies and commodity currencies.

### Terms of Trade
Countries that export commodities see their currencies rise when commodity prices rise (AUD with iron ore, CAD with oil, NOK with oil).

## The Carry Trade
One of the most popular macro strategies. Borrow in a low-interest-rate currency, invest in a high-interest-rate currency, pocket the difference (the "carry").

Classic example: Borrow in JPY at 0%, invest in AUD at 4%. Earn 4% carry.

**Risk:** If the high-yield currency falls sharply, the capital loss exceeds the carry earned. Carry trades unwind violently during risk-off events — this is called a "carry unwind."

## Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)
The long-run theory that exchange rates should adjust so that identical goods cost the same in different countries. In practice, currencies can deviate from PPP for years or decades. The Economist's Big Mac Index is a popular PPP measure.

## The Yen and Global Risk
The JPY is unique — it tends to strengthen during global risk-off events even though Japan has massive debt. This is because Japanese investors (who hold enormous overseas assets) repatriate capital during crises, creating yen demand. The JPY is one of the best hedges against global financial stress.

## Emerging Market Currencies
EM currencies are highly sensitive to:
- US dollar strength (most EM debt is dollar-denominated)
- Fed rate hikes (trigger capital outflows from EM)
- Global risk sentiment
- Commodity prices (for commodity-exporting EMs)
- Domestic political stability

## Bottom Line
Currency markets are the transmission mechanism for all macro forces. Interest rate differentials, growth differentials, and risk sentiment all express themselves through FX. The DXY is the single most important currency indicator for macro traders.
