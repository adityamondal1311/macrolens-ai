# Quantitative Tightening (QT)

## What It Is
Quantitative Tightening is the process by which a central bank reduces the size of its balance sheet — the reverse of Quantitative Easing. During QE, the central bank expanded its balance sheet by buying bonds with newly created money. During QT, it shrinks its balance sheet by removing that money from the system.

QT is sometimes called "balance sheet normalization" or "balance sheet runoff."

## How QT Works

### Passive QT (Runoff)
The most common approach. The central bank simply stops reinvesting the proceeds when its bonds mature:
1. A Treasury bond on the Fed's balance sheet matures
2. The Treasury pays the Fed the face value
3. The Fed does NOT use those proceeds to buy new bonds
4. The Fed's balance sheet shrinks by that amount
5. Bank reserves in the system decrease

### Active QT (Outright Sales)
The central bank actively sells bonds back into the market before maturity. More disruptive than passive QT because it adds supply to the market immediately, pushing yields higher.

The Fed has used passive QT in its post-2022 program. Active sales are rare and considered a more aggressive tool.

## The Fed's QT Programs

### QT1 (2017-2019)
- Started October 2017 at $10B/month runoff pace
- Gradually increased to $50B/month cap
- Ended in September 2019 after the repo market stress event
- Fed balance sheet reduced from ~$4.5T to ~$3.8T

### QT2 (2022-Present)
- Started June 2022 at $47.5B/month
- Increased to $95B/month cap (fastest in history)
- Response to post-COVID QE that expanded balance sheet to $9T
- Fed balance sheet reduced from $9T to ~$7T by 2024

## How QT Affects Markets

### Bond Markets
QT removes a major buyer from the Treasury market. With the Fed no longer reinvesting, the private sector must absorb more supply at the margin:
- Long-term Treasury yields face upward pressure
- Term premium rises as supply/demand balance shifts
- The effect is estimated at 15-25bps per $1T of QT (equivalent to roughly 1-2 rate hikes)

### Equity Markets
QT drains liquidity from the financial system:
- Less money chasing assets → lower asset prices
- Higher long-term yields → multiple compression for equities
- Tighter financial conditions → slower growth → earnings risk

### Bank Reserves
QT directly reduces bank reserves (the money banks hold at the Fed):
- Banks lend more cautiously as reserves decline
- Repo rates face upward pressure
- Financial conditions tighten even without rate moves

### Dollar
QT tends to be dollar-positive — it tightens domestic liquidity, raises yields, and reduces the supply of dollar-denominated financial assets available to global investors.

## The "Ample Reserves" Problem
The Fed aims to maintain "ample reserves" — enough bank reserves that the repo market functions smoothly without the Fed's intervention. The problem: nobody knows exactly where the "ample" threshold is until you've gone below it.

Going too far with QT risks a repeat of September 2019 — repo market stress from reserve scarcity. The Fed must constantly monitor reserve levels, repo rates, and money market conditions to calibrate QT.

## QT vs Rate Hikes: Which Is More Powerful?
Rate hikes directly affect the short end of the yield curve and transmit to borrowing costs. QT primarily affects the long end through supply/demand dynamics. They work through different channels but are complementary:

- Rate hikes: price of money (interest rates)
- QT: quantity of money (liquidity)

Combined aggressive rate hikes + QT represent the most restrictive monetary stance since the Volcker era.

## Reverse Repo Facility as QT Buffer
During QT, money market funds initially drew down their balances in the Fed's Reverse Repo (RRP) facility before bank reserves were affected. While RRP balances were declining, QT wasn't draining bank reserves — it was "stealth" tightening. Once RRP balances approach zero, QT starts directly hitting bank reserves, making the next phase more impactful.

## When Does QT End?
The Fed signals QT will slow or stop when:
- Bank reserves approach the "ample" threshold
- Repo markets show stress (rates spiking)
- Financial conditions tighten beyond desired levels
- Economic conditions warrant looser monetary policy

QT typically ends before rate cuts begin — the two tools are on different timelines.

## Global QT
The ECB and BoE also ran QT programs alongside the Fed post-2022. Simultaneous global QT represents a meaningful withdrawal of global liquidity — unprecedented in scale. The combined effect of major central banks all reducing balance sheets simultaneously is a structural headwind for global asset prices.

## Bottom Line
QT is the silent tightener. Its effects are less visible than rate hikes but no less real. Watch the Fed's balance sheet weekly, monitor bank reserve levels, and track repo rates for stress signals. When QT ends, it removes a meaningful headwind for long-duration bonds and risk assets.
