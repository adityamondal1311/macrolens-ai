# Global Capital Flows

## What They Are
Capital flows are the movement of money across borders for investment purposes — into stocks, bonds, real estate, direct business investment, and bank deposits. These flows are the transmission mechanism connecting monetary policy, interest rate differentials, and risk appetite to currency and asset markets.

## Types of Capital Flows

### Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
Long-term investment in productive assets — building factories, acquiring companies, establishing subsidiaries. Relatively stable, driven by long-term growth expectations and regulatory environment.

### Portfolio Investment
Purchase of financial assets — stocks and bonds — by foreign investors. More volatile than FDI. Macro traders focus on portfolio flows as they are large, fast-moving, and directly impact prices.

### Bank Lending and Credit Flows
Cross-border loans and credit lines from international banks. The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) tracks these. Global bank deleveraging (pulling back cross-border lending) during crises is a major source of financial stress.

### Official Flows
Central bank reserve management — foreign exchange reserves invested in bonds (primarily US Treasuries). China, Japan, Saudi Arabia, and other surplus nations recycle export earnings into US Treasuries, directly funding US deficits and suppressing yields.

## What Drives Capital Flows

### Interest Rate Differentials
The primary driver. Capital moves toward higher real returns:
```
Higher rates → Capital inflows → Currency appreciation → Bond yields suppressed
Lower rates → Capital outflows → Currency depreciation → Bond yields rise
```

### Growth Differentials
Faster-growing economies attract equity investment. When the US grows faster than Europe, capital flows into US equities and out of European assets.

### Risk Appetite
In risk-on environments, capital flows from safe havens (US, Japan, Switzerland) into higher-yielding EM and frontier markets. In risk-off, the reverse — sudden stops and reversals of EM capital flows create currency crises.

### Valuation
Cheap assets attract capital over the long run. Expensive valuations deter flows.

### Geopolitical and Regulatory Factors
Sanctions, capital controls, property rights, and political stability all influence where global capital chooses to go.

## Effects on Currencies
Capital flows directly determine exchange rates in the short and medium run:

- **Capital inflows:** Foreigners must buy local currency to invest → currency appreciates
- **Capital outflows:** Investors sell local currency → currency depreciates
- **Sudden stops:** Abrupt reversal of capital inflows → currency crisis (Southeast Asia 1997, EM 2013, 2022)

## Effects on Bond Yields
Foreign demand for a country's bonds is a critical determinant of yield levels:

- **Strong foreign demand for US Treasuries** → yields suppressed below where they'd otherwise be
- **Foreign selling of Treasuries** → yields spike (Japan selling Treasuries to defend yen is a recurring concern)
- **China's reserve accumulation** (2000s-2010s) was a major factor holding US yields low — the "global savings glut"

When foreign buyers retreat, domestic buyers must absorb supply at higher yields. This is why geopolitical tensions that reduce foreign Treasury demand are a macro risk.

## The Dollar Milkshake Theory
A framework suggesting that because the dollar is the global reserve currency, Fed tightening sucks capital from the rest of the world into dollar assets — like a milkshake straw draining capital globally. This explains why Fed hikes strengthen the dollar so aggressively and stress EM simultaneously.

## Balance of Payments
Every country's external account:
```
Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account = 0
```

- **Current account:** Trade in goods, services, income flows
- **Capital/Financial account:** Investment flows in and out

A current account deficit must be financed by capital account surplus — the country must attract foreign investment to pay for its trade deficit. When this financing dries up, a currency crisis can ensue.

## Macro Trade: Following Capital Flows
Macro traders track capital flow data to anticipate currency and asset moves:
- **TIC Data (Treasury International Capital):** Monthly US data on foreign purchases of US assets
- **IMF COFER:** Central bank reserve composition data
- **BIS International Banking Statistics:** Global bank flow data
- **EPFR Fund Flows:** Weekly fund flow data into equity and bond funds by country

## Bottom Line
Capital flows are the plumbing of the global financial system. Interest rate differentials pull the flows, risk appetite determines their velocity, and currencies adjust to balance supply and demand. When flows reverse suddenly, currencies and bond markets move violently. This is why the Fed's actions reverberate globally — every rate hike reshapes where capital wants to be.
