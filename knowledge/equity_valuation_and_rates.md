# Equity Valuation and Interest Rates

## The Core Relationship
Interest rates are the single biggest macro driver of equity market valuations. The entire framework of equity valuation is built on discounting future cash flows at a rate determined by interest rates. When rates rise, stocks become worth less — mathematically and mechanically.

## The Discounted Cash Flow Framework
A stock's intrinsic value is the present value of all future earnings:

```
Stock Value = E1/(1+r) + E2/(1+r)² + E3/(1+r)³ + ... + En/(1+r)^n
```

Where r = discount rate (risk-free rate + equity risk premium)

When interest rates rise, r rises, and the denominator grows — compressing present value. The further out the cash flows, the more dramatically they are discounted. This is why long-duration assets (growth stocks with earnings far in the future) are hit hardest by rising rates.

## The P/E Ratio and Rates
The price-to-earnings ratio is the simplest valuation metric:

```
P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share
```

The "fair" P/E is roughly the inverse of the discount rate:

```
Fair P/E ≈ 1 / (Risk-Free Rate + Equity Risk Premium)
```

When the 10Y yield is 1%, fair P/E ≈ 1/(0.01 + 0.04) = 20x
When the 10Y yield is 5%, fair P/E ≈ 1/(0.05 + 0.04) = 11x

This compression from 20x to 11x is pure multiple contraction — earnings don't have to change for stocks to fall 45%.

## Growth vs Value in Different Rate Environments

### Low Rate Environment (Rates Falling or Near Zero)
- **Growth stocks outperform dramatically**
- High P/E companies justified by low discount rates
- Long-duration cash flows worth more in present value terms
- FAANG/tech stocks thrive
- "TINA" (There Is No Alternative to equities) drives inflows

### High Rate Environment (Rates Rising)
- **Value stocks outperform relatively**
- Near-term cash flows preferred over distant earnings
- Low P/E companies with current profitability more attractive
- Financials benefit (wider net interest margins)
- Energy, industrials, healthcare outperform tech

## Technology Sector Sensitivity
Tech stocks are particularly rate-sensitive because:
1. Most tech companies reinvest earnings for years — cash flows are far in the future
2. Tech stocks trade at very high multiples (high P/E = high duration)
3. A small change in discount rate causes a large change in valuation

Example: A stock trading at 50x earnings has much greater duration than one at 10x. When rates rise 1%, the 50x stock might re-rate to 35x, a 30% fall in multiple alone — even if earnings don't change.

This explains why the Nasdaq fell ~35% in 2022 when the Fed hiked aggressively — it was almost entirely multiple contraction driven by rate increases.

## The Equity Risk Premium (ERP)
The excess return stocks offer over the risk-free rate to compensate for their higher risk:

```
ERP = Earnings Yield (1/PE) - 10Y Treasury Yield
```

When ERP compresses (stocks expensive relative to bonds), bonds become more attractive and equity valuations are vulnerable to correction.

In 2023-2024, the ERP in the US turned negative — meaning bonds yielded more than stocks on an earnings yield basis for the first time in decades. This is why many analysts warned about equity valuations despite strong earnings growth.

## The Fed Model
A simplified framework comparing the earnings yield of the S&P 500 to the 10Y Treasury yield:
- If earnings yield > bond yield: stocks are cheap relative to bonds
- If bond yield > earnings yield: bonds are cheap relative to stocks

While criticized for being simplistic, it captures the fundamental trade-off investors make between stocks and bonds.

## Rate Hike Cycles and Equity Markets
Historical pattern during Fed hiking cycles:
- **Early hikes (first 2-3):** Equities often hold up — hikes signal economic strength
- **Middle of cycle:** Equities can still grind higher if earnings grow
- **Late cycle (approaching peak rate):** Equities vulnerable as growth slows
- **Post-peak (before cuts):** Most dangerous period — peak restrictiveness

## Sector Rotation Based on Rates

| Rate Environment | Outperforming Sectors |
|---|---|
| Rates falling | Technology, utilities, REITs, growth |
| Rates rising | Financials, energy, industrials, value |
| Rates high and stable | Dividend stocks, healthcare, consumer staples |

## Bottom Line
Rising rates are the primary headwind for equity valuations. The mechanism is direct — higher discount rates compress P/E multiples. Growth stocks are most vulnerable (longest duration). Value and financials are relatively protected. Every macro trader must know where rates are going before sizing equity positions.
