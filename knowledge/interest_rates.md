# Interest Rates

## What They Are
Interest rates are the cost of borrowing money. They are the fundamental price in all of finance — every asset valuation model, every investment decision, every currency flow ultimately traces back to interest rates.

## Types of Interest Rates

### Policy Rate
Set directly by the central bank. In the US, this is the Federal Funds Rate — the rate at which banks lend to each other overnight. All other rates are built on top of this.

### Risk-Free Rate
US Treasury yields at various maturities. The baseline return available with zero credit risk. Every other investment is priced as a spread over this.

### SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate)
Replaced LIBOR as the global benchmark for floating rate debt. Reflects the cost of borrowing cash overnight collateralized by Treasury securities.

### Real Interest Rate
```
Real Rate = Nominal Rate - Inflation Expectations
```
The real rate is what actually matters for economic behavior. A 5% rate with 6% inflation is actually stimulative. A 3% rate with 1% inflation is restrictive.

## The Transmission Mechanism
How rate changes flow through the economy:

1. **Fed raises rates** → overnight borrowing costs rise
2. **Bank lending rates rise** → mortgages, auto loans, corporate loans get more expensive
3. **Consumer spending falls** → less borrowing for big purchases
4. **Business investment falls** → higher hurdle rate for projects
5. **Housing market slows** → mortgage rates tied directly to Treasury yields
6. **Unemployment eventually rises** → demand destruction slows inflation
7. **Inflation falls** → Fed achieves its goal

This process takes 12-18 months to fully transmit, which is why monetary policy is described as working with "long and variable lags."

## Interest Rate Differentials and Currency Markets
Perhaps the most direct market impact of interest rates is on currencies. Capital flows to where it earns the highest return:

```
Higher rates → Currency appreciates (carry inflows)
Lower rates → Currency depreciates (carry outflows)
```

This is the foundation of carry trades — borrowing in low-rate currencies and investing in high-rate currencies.

## Interest Rates and Equity Valuation
Equities are valued as the present value of future cash flows:
```
Stock Value = Sum of (Future Earnings / (1 + Discount Rate)^n)
```
When interest rates rise, the discount rate rises, reducing the present value of future earnings. Growth stocks with earnings far in the future are hit hardest. Value stocks with near-term cash flows are relatively protected.

## Neutral Rate (R*)
The theoretical interest rate that neither stimulates nor restricts the economy. Rates above neutral are restrictive; rates below neutral are stimulative. The Fed constantly tries to estimate R* — currently estimated at 2.5-3% in the US.

## Dot Plot
The Fed publishes projections of where each FOMC member expects rates to be in future years. This "dot plot" is released quarterly and is one of the most market-moving documents in finance.

## Rate Expectations and Market Pricing
Markets price future rate moves through Fed Funds Futures contracts. Macro traders constantly compare their rate expectations to market pricing to find opportunities. If you expect fewer cuts than the market is pricing, you want to be short bonds.

## Bottom Line
Interest rates are the price of money. Understanding where rates are, where they're going, and how fast they'll move is the core skill of macro trading. Everything else flows from this.
