# Term Premium

## What It Is
The term premium is the extra compensation investors demand for holding a long-term bond instead of rolling over a series of short-term bonds. It is the risk premium embedded in long-term yields that compensates for uncertainty about the future path of interest rates, inflation, and liquidity.

```
10Y Nominal Yield = Expected Average Short Rates over 10Y + Term Premium
```

## Why Term Premium Exists
Investors face more uncertainty over longer time horizons:
- **Inflation uncertainty:** Hard to predict inflation 10 years out
- **Interest rate uncertainty:** Future Fed policy is unpredictable
- **Liquidity risk:** Long bonds are harder to sell quickly without price impact
- **Duration risk:** Long bonds lose more value if yields rise

Investors rationally demand compensation for bearing these risks — this compensation is the term premium.

## Measuring Term Premium
Term premium cannot be directly observed — it must be estimated from models. The most widely followed is the **ACM (Adrian, Crump, Moench) Model** from the New York Fed.

A positive term premium means investors are being paid extra to hold long bonds. A negative term premium means investors are paying for the safety/liquidity of long bonds (accepting lower yields than short-term rate expectations alone would justify).

From 2010-2021, US term premium was persistently negative — investors were so desperate for safe long-duration assets (pension funds, insurance companies, foreign central banks) that they accepted yields below what short-rate expectations warranted.

## Why Term Premium Collapsed Post-2008
Several structural forces compressed term premium:
- **QE:** Fed buying long bonds directly reduced the supply available to private investors, driving prices up and yields/term premium down
- **Regulatory demand:** Basel III capital rules incentivized banks to hold government bonds
- **Global savings glut:** Surplus nations (China, Japan, Germany) recycled savings into US Treasuries
- **Pension fund demand:** Liability-matching demand for long bonds is structurally enormous
- **Low inflation volatility:** Stable inflation meant lower inflation uncertainty risk premium

## Term Premium Rising — The 2023 Episode
From mid-2023, US term premium rose sharply after years of being negative. Drivers:
- Fed QT reducing demand for long bonds
- Fiscal deficit expanding massively — more Treasury supply
- Higher inflation uncertainty after the 2021-2022 inflation surge
- Foreign central bank selling (Japan defending yen, China selling reserves)
- Reduced Fed credibility requiring extra inflation risk compensation

The 10Y yield surged to 5% in October 2023 largely due to rising term premium — not because short-rate expectations changed.

## Term Premium vs Rate Expectations
This decomposition matters enormously for macro positioning:

- If long yields are rising because **rate expectations are rising** → Fed is more hawkish than thought, short bonds aggressively
- If long yields are rising because **term premium is rising** → Supply/demand imbalance, fiscal concerns, inflation uncertainty — different implications for equities and currencies

Rising yields driven by term premium are generally more benign for equities (not signaling tighter near-term Fed policy) but more worrying long-term (fiscal sustainability concerns).

## Term Premium and the Yield Curve
Term premium is a major driver of yield curve slope. When term premium is:
- **High:** Yield curve steeper — investors demand significant extra compensation for duration
- **Low/Negative:** Yield curve flatter or inverted — short-rate expectations dominate

A curve inversion caused by rate expectations is a recession signal. A curve inversion caused by negative term premium (as in 2018-2019) is less reliable as a recession indicator.

## Global Term Premium Spillovers
US term premium affects global yields. When US term premium rises, global sovereign yields tend to rise in sympathy as investors globally demand higher compensation for duration risk. This transmission is particularly strong for developed market government bonds.

## Bottom Line
Term premium is the hidden variable in long-term yields. When the 10Y yield moves, decompose it: is it rate expectations or term premium? The answer fundamentally changes the macro interpretation and the correct trading response. Rising term premium driven by fiscal concerns is a new structural risk for the 2020s that wasn't present in the post-GFC era.
