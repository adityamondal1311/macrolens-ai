# Quantitative Easing and Quantitative Tightening

## What Is QE?
Quantitative Easing (QE) is a non-conventional monetary policy tool used when interest rates are already near zero and the central bank needs to provide additional stimulus. The central bank creates new money and uses it to purchase financial assets — primarily government bonds and mortgage-backed securities — from the open market.

## How QE Works
1. Fed creates new bank reserves (electronically)
2. Uses reserves to buy Treasuries and MBS from banks and investors
3. Sellers receive cash, reinvest in other assets (equities, corporate bonds, real estate)
4. Asset prices rise across the board (the "wealth effect")
5. Bond yields fall as the Fed absorbs supply
6. Lower yields encourage borrowing and investment
7. Dollar weakens as money supply expands

## QE's Effect on Asset Classes

### Bonds
Direct and immediate. Fed buying pushes prices up and yields down across the curve. The portfolio rebalancing effect pushes investors into riskier assets.

### Equities
QE inflates equity valuations through two channels:
- Lower discount rates increase the present value of future earnings
- Portfolio rebalancing forces money into equities as bonds become expensive

### Gold
QE is historically very bullish for gold because it raises fears of currency debasement and keeps real yields negative.

### US Dollar
QE weakens the dollar by expanding the money supply and keeping rates low. This is bullish for emerging market assets and commodities priced in dollars.

### Emerging Markets
Dollar weakness from QE sends capital flooding into EM assets seeking higher yields. The reversal of this (QT or rate hikes) causes EM crises as capital flees.

## History of QE Programs
- **QE1 (2008-2010):** $1.75 trillion. Response to Global Financial Crisis
- **QE2 (2010-2011):** $600 billion. To prevent deflation
- **QE3 (2012-2014):** $85 billion/month open-ended. Tied to unemployment threshold
- **COVID QE (2020):** Unlimited. Fed balance sheet went from $4T to $9T in two years

## What Is QT?
Quantitative Tightening (QT) is the reverse — the central bank shrinks its balance sheet by:
1. **Passive QT:** Letting bonds mature without reinvesting the proceeds
2. **Active QT:** Actively selling bonds back into the market (more disruptive)

## QT's Effect on Markets
QT removes liquidity from the financial system. Effects are the opposite of QE:
- Bond yields rise (less Fed demand, more supply hitting the market)
- Equities face valuation pressure
- Dollar strengthens
- Financial conditions tighten
- Credit spreads widen

## The Taper Tantrum (2013)
When Bernanke hinted the Fed might slow QE3 purchases in May 2013, 10Y Treasury yields spiked from 1.6% to 3% in months. Emerging market currencies collapsed. This became known as the "Taper Tantrum" — a lesson in how sensitive markets are to changes in Fed liquidity.

## Criticisms of QE
- Inflates asset prices disproportionately benefiting the wealthy
- Creates asset bubbles
- Diminishing returns with each successive program
- Makes it harder for central banks to normalize policy

## Bottom Line
QE and QT are the blunt instruments of monetary policy when rates hit zero. The size of central bank balance sheets is now a key variable macro traders monitor alongside interest rates. Balance sheet expansion = liquidity injection = risk-on. Balance sheet contraction = liquidity drain = risk-off.
