# Emerging Markets

## What Are Emerging Markets?
Emerging markets (EM) are economies transitioning from developing to developed status. Key EM blocs include the BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa), Southeast Asia, Eastern Europe, Latin America, and the Middle East.

## Why EM Matters in Macro
EM countries represent over 50% of global GDP and nearly all of global growth at the margin. China alone accounts for roughly 30% of global commodity demand. EM financial crises tend to be sudden, violent, and contagious.

## Key Drivers of EM Assets

### The US Dollar (Most Important)
The single biggest variable for EM is dollar direction. Most EM governments and corporations borrow in dollars because it's cheaper. When the dollar strengthens:
- Dollar-denominated debt becomes harder to service
- Capital flees EM back to the US for higher yields
- EM currencies weaken, importing inflation
- EM central banks forced to hike rates defensively even as growth slows

### Federal Reserve Policy
Fed rate hikes trigger EM outflows. The "Taper Tantrum" of 2013 and the 2022 hiking cycle both caused significant EM stress. The transmission: higher US rates → stronger dollar → EM capital outflows → EM currency weakness → EM financial stress.

### Global Growth and Risk Appetite
EM assets are high-beta risk assets. They outperform strongly in risk-on environments and underperform sharply in risk-off. When global growth is strong, demand for EM exports rises and investors seek EM yield.

### Commodity Prices
Many EM economies are commodity exporters:
- Brazil: soybeans, iron ore, oil
- Russia: oil, gas, metals
- South Africa: gold, platinum
- Chile: copper
- Gulf states: oil

Rising commodity prices improve EM terms of trade, strengthen currencies, and boost fiscal revenues.

### China
China is the demand engine for most commodity-exporting EMs. Chinese credit cycles drive Australian, Brazilian, and Chilean asset prices. When China stimulates (infrastructure, property), commodity demand surges. When China tightens, commodity-linked EMs suffer.

## EM Vulnerability Indicators
Macro traders assess EM vulnerability through:
- **Current account deficit:** Reliance on foreign capital to fund imports
- **Foreign currency reserves:** Ability to defend the currency
- **External debt as % of GDP:** Exposure to dollar strength
- **Inflation rate:** Domestic purchasing power erosion
- **Political stability:** Policy credibility

The "fragile five" framework (used in 2013) identified the most vulnerable EMs as those with large current account deficits: India, Brazil, Indonesia, Turkey, South Africa.

## Famous EM Crises

### Asian Financial Crisis (1997)
Fixed exchange rate regimes collapsed when dollar-denominated debt became unsustainable. Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea devastated. IMF bailouts. Lesson: currency pegs + dollar debt = crisis risk.

### Russian Default (1998)
Russia defaulted on domestic debt and devalued the ruble. Triggered global contagion, caused the collapse of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM).

### Taper Tantrum (2013)
Fed hinting at QE tapering caused massive EM capital outflows and currency crises across multiple EMs.

### Turkish Lira Crisis (2018, 2021)
Combination of current account deficits, dollar-denominated debt, political interference in central bank independence, and unorthodox monetary policy (cutting rates with high inflation) caused the lira to collapse.

## Trading EM Assets
- **EM Equities:** High beta to global risk. MSCI EM Index is the benchmark.
- **EM Local Currency Bonds:** Benefit from EM currency appreciation and rate cuts
- **EM Hard Currency Bonds (USD-denominated):** Credit risk without currency risk
- **EM FX:** Currencies like BRL, INR, TRY, ZAR, MXN

## Bottom Line
EM is essentially a leveraged bet on global growth, dollar weakness, and risk appetite. The dollar is the most important variable. When the Fed pivots dovish and the dollar weakens, go long EM. When the Fed hikes and the dollar surges, reduce EM exposure.
