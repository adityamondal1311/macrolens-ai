# Dollar Dominance

## The US Dollar as Global Reserve Currency
The US dollar is the world's reserve currency — the currency in which global trade is invoiced, commodities are priced, and central bank reserves are held. This status gives the US extraordinary privilege and makes the dollar the single most important variable in global macro.

Approximately:
- 88% of all FX transactions involve the dollar
- 60% of global central bank reserves are held in dollars
- 50% of global trade is invoiced in dollars
- Nearly all commodity markets (oil, gold, copper) price in dollars

## The Bretton Woods System
After World War II, the Bretton Woods agreement (1944) made the dollar the anchor of the global monetary system, pegged to gold at $35/oz. All other currencies were pegged to the dollar. This established dollar dominance officially.

In 1971, Nixon ended the gold convertibility of the dollar (the "Nixon Shock"), but dollar dominance persisted because of the petrodollar system.

## The Petrodollar System
The arrangement (formalized with Saudi Arabia in 1974) where oil is priced and traded globally in US dollars. In exchange for dollar invoicing, the US provided military protection to Gulf states.

This created structural dollar demand:
1. Any country needing to buy oil must first acquire dollars
2. Oil exporters receive dollars, recycle them into US Treasuries (petrodollar recycling)
3. US runs current account deficit, exports dollars, imports goods
4. Rest of world holds dollar reserves

The petrodollar system is why the US can run persistent trade deficits without a dollar crisis — there is always demand for dollars globally.

## Exorbitant Privilege
A term coined by French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing. The US can borrow in its own currency at lower rates than any other nation because global demand for dollars is structurally guaranteed. The US can also run large deficits without triggering the currency crises that would afflict other nations.

This privilege also means US monetary policy has outsized global effects — when the Fed hikes, the entire world feels it through dollar strengthening and capital flow reversals.

## Dollar Liquidity and Global Stress
Because so much global borrowing is dollar-denominated, dollar shortages cause global financial stress. When the dollar surges:
- Dollar-denominated debt becomes harder to service globally
- EM countries face balance of payments stress
- Global liquidity tightens even without Fed action

The Fed's dollar swap lines with other major central banks (ECB, BoJ, BoE, etc.) are emergency mechanisms to provide dollar liquidity during crises. Their activation signals severe global financial stress.

## De-Dollarization Attempts
Countries have long sought to reduce dollar dependence, accelerating after 2022 Russia sanctions froze dollar reserves:
- **BRICS payment systems:** Efforts to trade in local currencies
- **Chinese yuan internationalization:** Yuan-denominated oil contracts (petro-yuan)
- **Central bank gold buying:** Diversifying away from dollar reserves
- **Digital currencies:** CBDCs potentially bypassing dollar-based SWIFT system

However, network effects make dollar replacement extremely difficult. No alternative has the depth, liquidity, and legal infrastructure of dollar markets.

## The Dollar Smile Theory
Stephen Jen's framework: The dollar strengthens at both extremes:
1. **US outperformance:** Strong US growth attracts capital inflows → dollar rises
2. **Global risk-off/crisis:** Safe haven demand → dollar rises
3. **Middle (global expansion, US not outperforming):** Dollar weakens

This explains why the dollar often strengthens during global recessions and crises even though this seems counterintuitive for a country with large deficits.

## Dollar Strength and Global Macro Implications

**Strong Dollar:**
- Commodities (priced in dollars) fall in price
- EM currencies weaken, EM dollar debt stress rises
- US multinational earnings fall (overseas revenue worth less)
- Import prices fall for US (deflationary)
- Capital flows from EM to US

**Weak Dollar:**
- Commodities rally
- EM assets outperform
- US multinational earnings rise
- Import inflation rises for US
- Global liquidity increases

## Bottom Line
The dollar is the world's financial operating system. Its strength or weakness determines the tide for all global assets — especially commodities and emerging markets. Dollar dominance means US monetary policy is de facto global monetary policy. Every macro trader must have a view on the dollar.
