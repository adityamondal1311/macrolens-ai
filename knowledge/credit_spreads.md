# Credit Spreads

## What They Are
Credit spreads are the difference in yield between a corporate bond (or other non-government bond) and a comparable maturity government bond (the risk-free rate). The spread compensates investors for taking on credit risk — the risk that the borrower defaults.

```
Credit Spread = Corporate Bond Yield - Treasury Yield (same maturity)
```

Example: If a 10Y investment-grade corporate bond yields 5.5% and the 10Y Treasury yields 4.5%, the credit spread is 100 basis points (1%).

## Why Credit Spreads Matter in Macro
Credit spreads are one of the best real-time indicators of financial market stress and economic conditions. They reflect:
- Probability of corporate default
- Investor risk appetite
- Liquidity conditions
- Economic cycle positioning

When spreads widen sharply, financial conditions tighten dramatically — borrowing becomes more expensive for corporations, investment falls, and recession risk rises.

## Credit Rating Categories

### Investment Grade (IG)
Rated BBB- or above by S&P/Fitch (Baa3 or above by Moody's). Lower default risk, lower spread.
- AAA: ~20-50bps spread
- AA: ~30-70bps
- A: ~50-100bps
- BBB: ~100-200bps (widest IG category — most sensitive to cycles)

### High Yield (HY) / Junk
Rated BB+ or below. Higher default risk, higher spread.
- BB: ~200-400bps
- B: ~300-600bps
- CCC: ~600-1200bps+

## Key Credit Spread Indices
- **CDX IG:** Most liquid IG credit derivative index (North America)
- **CDX HY:** High yield credit derivative index
- **iTraxx Europe:** European IG credit index
- **Bloomberg US Corporate OAS:** Option-adjusted spread for US IG corporates
- **Bloomberg US HY OAS:** High yield spread index

## Credit Spread Regimes

### Tight Spreads (Risk-On)
- IG spreads below 100bps, HY below 350bps
- Signals strong credit market confidence
- Economic expansion, low default expectations
- Encourages corporate borrowing and M&A

### Normal Spreads
- IG: 100-150bps, HY: 350-550bps
- Mid-cycle conditions

### Wide Spreads (Risk-Off / Stress)
- IG above 200bps, HY above 700-800bps
- Signals financial stress and recession risk
- Peak crisis levels: IG 600bps+ (2008), HY 2000bps+ (2008)

## Credit Spreads as Leading Indicators
Credit markets often lead equity markets in signaling trouble. Corporate bond investors are typically more sophisticated than retail equity investors and react faster to deteriorating fundamentals.

Historical pattern:
1. Credit spreads begin widening (early warning)
2. Equity markets initially ignore credit stress
3. Eventually equities sell off sharply
4. Credit spreads peak as recession hits
5. Credit spreads tighten before equity market bottoms

## The High-Yield Spread as Recession Predictor
HY spreads above 700-800bps have historically coincided with or preceded US recessions. The logic: when high-yield companies face dramatically higher borrowing costs, they cut investment, hiring, and operations — directly contracting economic activity.

## Credit Spreads and the Fed
The Fed monitors credit spreads closely as part of financial conditions. If spreads blow out despite Fed rate cuts, it means the cuts aren't transmitting — financial conditions are tightening regardless. This was a concern during the GFC when the Fed cut rates to zero but credit markets remained frozen.

## Investment Grade vs High Yield Divergence
Sometimes IG and HY spreads move differently — this divergence is informative:
- IG tight but HY widening → early stress in riskier credits, watch for contagion
- Both tightening → broad risk appetite improvement
- IG widening but HY stable → unusual, often signals rate concern rather than credit concern

## CDS (Credit Default Swaps)
Derivatives that function as insurance against default. The CDS spread is the annual cost of buying protection on $10 million of debt. CDS markets often move before cash bond markets and provide real-time credit stress signals.

**Sovereign CDS:** Measures default risk on government debt. Key during European sovereign crisis (Greek CDS at 10,000bps in 2012).

## The Credit-Equity Relationship
Credit and equity markets are linked — both are claims on corporate cash flows:
- Tightening credit spreads → equity rally (shared risk appetite)
- Widening credit spreads → equity selloff (credit stress precedes equity stress)
- Divergence (equities rallying while credit widens) → warning signal

## Bottom Line
Credit spreads are the canary in the coal mine for macro stress. When HY spreads blow out above 700bps, reduce risk aggressively. When spreads are compressing from elevated levels, it's one of the best signals to add risk. Monitor the CDX HY index daily as a real-time gauge of financial market health.
