# Gold and Commodities

## Gold as a Macro Asset
Gold is unique — it is simultaneously a commodity, a currency, and a store of value. It has no yield, no cash flows, and no industrial utility that would justify its price. Its value comes entirely from collective belief in it as a monetary anchor.

## What Drives Gold

### Real Interest Rates (Most Important)
Gold has the strongest inverse correlation with real yields of any asset. When real rates (nominal yields minus inflation expectations) fall, gold rises. When real rates rise, gold falls.

```
Gold rises when: Nominal rates fall OR Inflation rises faster than nominal rates
Gold falls when: Nominal rates rise faster than inflation
```

This is why gold rallied during COVID QE (real rates went deeply negative) and sold off when the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022 (real rates surged).

### US Dollar
Gold is priced in dollars globally. A weaker dollar makes gold cheaper for foreign buyers, increasing demand. Strong inverse correlation with DXY.

### Geopolitical Risk and Safe Haven Demand
Gold spikes during wars, financial crises, and geopolitical uncertainty. Central banks accelerated gold purchases after 2022 Russia sanctions froze dollar reserves — gold cannot be sanctioned.

### Central Bank Buying
Emerging market central banks (China, India, Turkey, Poland) have been significant gold buyers, diversifying away from dollar reserves. This is a structural demand driver.

### Inflation Hedge (Overstated)
Gold is commonly cited as an inflation hedge, but the relationship is inconsistent in the short run. It works better as a hedge against extreme inflation scenarios and currency debasement than as a monthly inflation tracker.

## Key Commodities in Macro

### Oil (WTI and Brent)
- **Supply drivers:** OPEC+ production decisions, US shale output, geopolitical disruptions
- **Demand drivers:** Global GDP growth (especially China), seasonal patterns
- **Macro relevance:** Oil is a tax on consumers. Rising oil → higher CPI → Fed pressure. Oil-exporting country currencies (CAD, NOK, RUB) are tightly correlated.
- **Brent vs WTI:** Brent is global benchmark; WTI is US benchmark. Spread reflects US export dynamics.

### Copper
Known as "Dr. Copper" because of its predictive power for global economic health. Copper is used in construction, manufacturing, and electronics — high demand signals real economic activity.
- Rising copper = bullish global growth signal
- Falling copper = warning sign for slowdown, especially China

### Natural Gas
Highly regional market (unlike oil). European gas prices and US Henry Hub prices can diverge dramatically. Weather-sensitive. Became a major geopolitical variable after Russia-Ukraine conflict.

## The Commodity Supercycle
Commodities tend to move in long multi-decade cycles driven by:
1. Underinvestment in supply during bear markets
2. Rising demand from EM industrialization (China 2000s)
3. Energy transition demand (copper, lithium, nickel for EVs)

Some analysts argue we entered a new commodity supercycle post-2020 driven by underinvestment, ESG constraints on fossil fuel capex, and green energy transition demand.

## Commodities and the Dollar Relationship
Most commodities are priced in USD. When the dollar strengthens:
- Commodities become more expensive in local currencies globally
- Demand falls → commodity prices fall
- Commodity-exporting EM currencies weaken

When the dollar weakens, the opposite — commodities rally, EM currencies strengthen.

## Bottom Line
Gold is the purest expression of real rate expectations and dollar weakness. Oil and copper are the best real-time indicators of global growth. Every macro trader monitors commodity markets as a cross-check on their economic thesis.
