# Fiscal Policy

## What It Is
Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Unlike monetary policy (controlled by the central bank), fiscal policy is set by elected governments. It is increasingly important in macro trading as governments run historically large deficits.

## Key Fiscal Concepts

### Government Spending
Direct injection of money into the economy. Categories:
- **Discretionary spending:** Annually appropriated (defense, infrastructure, education)
- **Mandatory spending:** Automatic programs (Social Security, Medicare, interest on debt)
- **Transfer payments:** Redistribution (unemployment benefits, stimulus checks)

Increased government spending is directly stimulative — it adds to GDP via the spending multiplier. The multiplier effect: $1 of government spending can generate more than $1 of economic activity as it circulates through the economy.

### Budget Deficit
When government spending exceeds tax revenues:
```
Deficit = Government Spending - Tax Revenue
```
The US runs persistent deficits — currently over $2 trillion annually. Deficits must be financed by issuing Treasury bonds, adding to the national debt and increasing bond supply in the market.

### National Debt and Debt-to-GDP
The accumulated stock of past deficits. US national debt exceeds $34 trillion (approximately 120% of GDP). High debt-to-GDP ratios create concerns about:
- Long-term fiscal sustainability
- Crowding out private investment
- Eventual monetization (central bank buying debt to suppress yields)
- Sovereign credit risk in extreme cases

### Fiscal Multiplier
The economic impact of a change in government spending. If the multiplier is 1.5, a $100 billion spending increase generates $150 billion of GDP growth.

Multipliers are higher when:
- The economy is in recession (slack capacity)
- Interest rates are at zero (no crowding out effect)
- Spending goes to lower-income households (higher marginal propensity to consume)

## Fiscal Stimulus and Market Impact
Large fiscal stimulus is inflationary and pushes yields higher:

1. Government borrows heavily → increases Treasury supply
2. More supply pushes bond yields higher
3. Higher yields tighten financial conditions
4. But stimulus also boosts growth and corporate earnings
5. Net effect: higher yields + higher equities (initially)

The 2021 American Rescue Plan ($1.9 trillion) combined with prior COVID stimulus is widely credited with contributing to the 2021-2023 inflation surge.

## Austerity
The opposite of stimulus — cutting spending and/or raising taxes to reduce deficits. Contractionary in the short run:
- Reduces aggregate demand
- Can trigger recession if poorly timed
- Lowers bond supply → yields fall
- Weakens growth but reduces inflation pressure

The 2010-2013 European austerity programs deepened the eurozone recession and nearly broke the euro.

## Fiscal Dominance
A dangerous regime where fiscal deficits are so large that the central bank effectively loses its independence — it must keep rates low or buy government bonds to prevent a debt crisis, even if inflation is high. Markets price fiscal dominance risk through:
- Higher long-term bond yields (term premium)
- Currency weakness
- Gold outperformance

The UK's September 2022 "mini-budget" (unfunded tax cuts) is a recent example — gilts and sterling collapsed simultaneously, forcing the BoE to intervene.

## How Macro Traders Assess Fiscal Policy
- **Primary balance:** Deficit excluding interest payments — measures underlying fiscal trajectory
- **Debt maturity profile:** When does existing debt need to be refinanced?
- **Deficit trajectory:** Is the deficit growing or shrinking?
- **Political credibility:** Will announced fiscal plans actually be implemented?

## Interaction with Monetary Policy
Fiscal and monetary policy can work together or against each other:
- **Coordinated easing:** Both stimulating (COVID response) — very powerful, highly inflationary
- **Coordinated tightening:** Both restrictive — aggressive demand destruction
- **Policy mix conflict:** Loose fiscal + tight monetary (current US situation) — higher for longer rates, upward pressure on long yields
- **Loose monetary + tight fiscal:** Less common, European post-GFC period

## Bottom Line
Fiscal policy is now as important as monetary policy for macro traders. Large fiscal deficits mean more Treasury issuance, which pressures long-end yields higher regardless of Fed policy. Watch deficit projections, Congressional Budget Office forecasts, and political developments around spending bills.
